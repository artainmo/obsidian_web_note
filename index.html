What can you do without being a MD?

I asked chatGPT: "I write medical research like this one: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.26599/BSA.2024.905006. However, I am not an official medical doctor. This is why I received he title 'consultant' on that research article. I even performed reviews for medical journals of Elsevier as you can see on my ORCID: https://orcid.org/0009-0009-0781-684X. Is it even legal to do medical research without being an official medical doctor?", and chatGPT answered: "
The short answer is: yes, it is legal to do medical research without being a medical doctor, but there are important boundaries and conditions.
1. Medical Research vs. Medical Practice
Medical practice (diagnosing, prescribing, treating patients) generally requires a medical license (e.g., MD, DO). Medical research (designing studies, analyzing data, writing papers, reviewing manuscripts) does not require you to be a licensed physician. Many medical researchers come from biology, biostatistics, public health, biomedical engineering, or related fields.
2. Who Can Publish Medical Research
Journals care about scientific merit, methodology, and peer review, not whether the author holds a medical degree. Titles such as 'consultant', 'researcher', 'scientist', or 'PhD candidate' are often used when authors are not MDs. As long as your role and credentials are accurately represented, it is legitimate.
4. Peer Review Without Being a Doctor
Journals recruit reviewers based on expertise, not on medical licensing. If your background matches the subject (e.g., data science, pharmacology, molecular biology), you may be invited to review. This is completely legitimate, provided you disclose your expertise honestly.
5. Legal Risks
Not illegal: Writing papers, analyzing data, publishing, or reviewing.
Illegal (in most countries): Presenting yourself as a licensed physician when you are not, or practicing medicine without a license.
As long as you don’t misrepresent your qualifications, you are safe.
6. Conclusions
It is perfectly legal for you to do and publish medical research without being a medical doctor. Many researchers in biomedical fields are not MDs. What matters is honesty about your role and following ethical guidelines (especially for studies involving patients or clinical data).
".

1. 'Consultant' in Research
In academic publishing, 'consultant' usually just means someone who contributed expert knowledge in a specialized field (e.g., statistics, bioinformatics, biomedical engineering, medical writing). It does not imply medical licensure or the ability to provide patient care.
2. 'Consultant' in Medicine (Clinical Context)
In the UK, Ireland, and some Commonwealth countries, 'Consultant' is a formal title for a senior physician who has completed all specialist training. In that sense, it explicitly means 'medical doctor at the top of their specialty'. Using that title in this way without an MD and specialist certification would be misleading and possibly unlawful.
3. Can a Non-MD Give Medical Advice?
No. Unless you are a licensed physician (or in some cases, a licensed healthcare provider like a nurse practitioner, physician assistant, or clinical psychologist depending on jurisdiction), you cannot legally give personalized medical advice or treatment plans to patients. However, non-MDs can consult on research projects, data analysis, medical writing, or regulatory aspects of studies. This is legitimate as long as it’s clear that the consultation is scientific/technical, not clinical care.
4. Best Practice for You
Always clarify in publications that your role as 'consultant' is research/technical. If you are asked to review papers or contribute to projects, present yourself as a scientific consultant/researcher, not a medical consultant. Never use 'Consultant Physician' or similar titles unless you are formally licensed as such.
5. Conclusions:
A non-MD can absolutely be called a consultant in a research paper — it means you contributed expertise, not that you are treating patients.

If the research requires medical judgment or intervention (e.g., prescribing drugs, performing surgery, making diagnoses), an MD must be involved.
If the research is observational, survey-based, data-driven, laboratory-based, or device-related, then a non-MD can often be the lead researcher — provided ethics approvals and institutional rules are followed.

Case reports/case studies usually describe a treatment or event that already happened in the course of normal medical care.

Approved drugs (on the market): used in 'off-label' ways sometimes — but only if prescribed by a licensed physician.

Only licensed clinicians can test drugs directly on patients.

A non-MD cannot 'try a drug on a patient' for research — that’s clinical practice and requires a medical license. Where can MDs practice?

Subsequently I asked chatGPT in the same discussion: "Can a Belgian doctor prescribe medications to patients in other countries?", and he answered: "
The short answer is: generally no, a Belgian doctor cannot legally prescribe medications to patients in other countries, unless very specific conditions are met.
1. Medical Licensure is National
Medical licenses are typically valid only in the country (or region) where they were issued.
A Belgian doctor is licensed to practice medicine in Belgium (and possibly in certain EU countries under mutual recognition agreements).
Prescribing for patients abroad usually requires a local medical license, otherwise it may be considered illegal practice of medicine without a license in the patient’s country.
2. EU Specifics
Within the EU/EEA, there are some harmonized rules.
Prescriptions from one EU country can sometimes be recognized in another.
3. Outside the EU
Most non-EU countries (e.g., U.S., Canada, Asian countries) do not recognize EU prescriptions at all. A Belgian doctor writing a prescription for a patient living in the U.S. would have no legal effect — pharmacies there would reject it.
4. Telemedicine Across Borders
Cross-border telemedicine is a legal gray zone.
In Belgium (and most EU countries), a doctor is only supposed to treat patients who are physically present in Belgium or under Belgian care.
5. Practical Reality
If a Belgian doctor wants to treat patients abroad, they usually need: local licensure (or temporary registration in that country).
6. Conclusion:
A Belgian doctor cannot normally prescribe medication directly to patients in other countries, unless it’s within the EU under limited recognition rules (and even then, pharmacies may reject it). For regular treatment, patients must have prescriptions from a doctor licensed in their own country.
".

You can ask your doctor to give you a prescription to use in another EU country, also known as a "cross-border prescription".

Subsequently I asked chatGPT in the same discussion: "If you are a Belgian doctor, can you easily get a local licensure to prescribe in the US for example?", and he answered: "
That’s a very good (and common) question among internationally trained doctors. Unfortunately, the answer is: no, it is not easy for a Belgian (or any foreign-trained) doctor to get licensed to prescribe in the U.S.
1. U.S. Licensure Basics
In the U.S., each state regulates medical practice and licensure.
To prescribe medications, you need: a state medical license (from the state’s medical board); a DEA registration (from the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration) if you will prescribe controlled substances.
2. Pathway for Foreign-Trained Doctors (e.g., from Belgium)
First, Credential verification via the Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates (ECFMG) will check that your medical degree is valid and from a recognized institution.
Second, you must pass the USMLE (United States Medical Licensing Examination).
Third you need residency in US. Even if you are already a specialist in Belgium, most foreign doctors must repeat at least part of U.S. residency training. This is often the biggest hurdle (competitive and time-consuming).
Fourth, after completing residency and exams, you apply for a license in the state where you want to practice.
Lastly you can register with the DEA to prescribe controlled substances.
4. Practical Reality
A Belgian doctor would usually need to go through years of exams and U.S. training before being able to prescribe in the U.S.
"

2. Jurisdiction in Cross-Border Cases
If you give advice to someone in the U.S., for example, U.S. regulators could theoretically claim jurisdiction because the effect (the advice) occurred within the U.S. In practice, prosecuting a foreign individual living abroad is complex and rare, unless the case causes significant harm or public health risk.

3. Real-World Enforcement
Most cross-border enforcement happens in serious cases (fraud, harm, large-scale illegal telemedicine operations).

However, Belgium can still hold you accountable if you present yourself as a medical practitioner without a Belgian license, even if the patient is not Belgian.

You are not 'protected' just because you live in Belgium.
